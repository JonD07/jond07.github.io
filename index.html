Bio

I am a PhD student in Robotics at Colorado School of Mines. I am a member of the Pervasive Computing Systems (PeCS) research group, working under Dr. Qi Han. My research interests are in autonomous robots with a focus on path planning for UAVs. I also have a general interest in artificial intelligence, math optimization, and smartgrid control. Prior to starting at Mines, I completed a bachelor's degree in Computer Science with minors in Mathematics and Mechatronics Technologies at Penn State Harrisburg. Prior to starting postsecondary education, I severed in the United States Marine Corps for five years.

In my free time I enjoy skiing, golfing, hiking, and playing video games. I'm also an avid traveler and love to explore new places and try new foods.

Current Research

My current research is focused on path planning for UAVs where the launch and receive sites are dynamic, integrated communication and control for swarms of UAVs, and building robust UAV testbeds for evaluating algorithms.

Minimizing Time while On-The-Move

In many UAV applications, it is more advantageous for a UAV base station to move to a new location after launching UAVs as opposed to remaining in the same position while the UAV performs some given task. In this project we are looking at how plan UAV routes that minimize mission completion time while accounting for a moving base station. We also consider the relationship between UAV speed and power consumption and look at how scheduling speed can effect route planning.

SmallSat

In this project we are looking at how to integrate communication and control in swarms of UAVs. We use the NEAR-Shoemaker mission as a motivating case study and look at how using a swarm of small satellites could be used to optimize the quantity and quality of data collected from the asteroid Eros.

Previous Projects

Load-Leveling on Cyber-Physical Microgrid

Improving the reliability and efficiency of microgrids to handle diverse load types will lead to more dependable electrical grids in small communities, reduce costs for both consumers and suppliers, and reduce carbon emissions. This work propose a hardware implementation of demand side management (DSM) on an actual microgrid system to support grid needs. We proposed an algorithm for achieving DSM through load-leveling at the micro-level and implemented it on a physical microgrid. We conducted a simulated case study, based on the electrical load needs of small communities in Bangladesh, to demonstrate how our algorithm can be used to better balance electrical load demands.

Sinusoidal PWM for Three-Phase Converter

Sinusoidal Pulse width modulation (sPWM) is the most commonly used technology for switching power electronics-based power converters in smart grids. This work looked at how to implement sPWM for single-phase and three-phase silicon carbide (SiC) converters using digital signal processing boards from Texas Instruments. We implemented our sPWM signal for SiC converters on a C2000 F28069M LaunchPad development kit and used it on a 3.5 kW three-phase SiC inverter experimental setup to validate the three-phase sPWM implementation.
